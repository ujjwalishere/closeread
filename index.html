In the early twentieth century, glaciers covered vast stretches of our mountain ranges. They fed rivers, sustained agriculture, and anchored entire ecosystems.

But decade by decade, the ice has retreated. What once seemed permanent is now visibly shrinking.

The scale of loss is difficult to grasp from a single photograph. To understand it, we need to look at the numbers.

Climate Change and Glaciers

Climate change is causing glaciers to melt at an accelerating rate due to rising global temperatures. As atmospheric and surface temperatures increase, glaciers lose more ice through melting than they accumulate from annual snowfall, resulting in their rapid retreat and shrinkage. This process is particularly severe in mountain regions like the Himalayas, where glaciers are sensitive to even small temperature changes. The consequences are far-reaching: reduced glacier volume threatens freshwater supplies for billions of people who depend on glacial meltwater for drinking water, irrigation, and hydroelectric power. Additionally, glacier loss contributes to sea-level rise, alters regional precipitation patterns, and destabilizes mountain ecosystems that have evolved in harmony with these frozen landscapes.

Glacial retreat doesn’t happen in isolation. It cascades through ecosystems, from alpine meadows to polar seas.

Polar bears depend on sea ice for hunting. As ice platforms disappear earlier each spring, hunting seasons shorten and survival rates fall.

The consequences ripple outward — through food webs, migration patterns, and Indigenous communities whose cultures are tied to the ice.

Scientists have been sounding the alarm for decades. The words of glaciologists carry a particular weight — they have watched their subject of study vanish before their eyes.

Consider this reflection from the field. It captures the emotional dimension of what is, at its core, a measurement problem.

When your data points are disappearing, the science becomes personal.

What can we do?

The science is unambiguous: glaciers are retreating, and the pace is accelerating. But the story is not yet finished. Reducing emissions, protecting watersheds, and supporting communities on the front lines of ice loss are concrete steps we can take.
